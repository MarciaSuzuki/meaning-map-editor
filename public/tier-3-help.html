Tier 2 Categories Guide

Meaning Map Editor • Tripod Ontology v5.4

This guide explains the categories that require human judgment—the fields that cannot be automatically filled from the Hebrew text database. These are where your insight as an analyst makes the difference.

How to Use This Guide

Each category includes: (1) a simple definition, (2) the available values, and (3) a biblical example showing how to apply it. When in doubt, choose "not_specified"—it is always better to leave a field blank than to guess wrong.

Tip: The pre-fill engine handles WHO, WHAT, and basic WHEN/WHERE. Your job is to add the WHY, HOW IT FEELS, and WHAT IT MEANS.

1. Participant Properties

Properties describe what participants (people, places, things) are like. These help match the biblical text with similar descriptions in the Language Archive.

Physical Properties

Observable characteristics of a person or thing—age, health, size, strength.

Values: old, young, strong, weak, beautiful, tall, short, big, small, heavy, light, fat, thin, healthy, sick, alive, deceased, not_specified, other

Example: Genesis 18:11 — "Abraham and Sarah were old, advanced in years." → Tag Abraham and Sarah with old.

Example: 1 Samuel 17:42 — "David was ruddy, with beautiful eyes and a handsome appearance." → Tag David with young, beautiful.

Sensory Properties

How something is perceived through the senses—sight, sound, smell, taste, touch, temperature.

Values: bright, dark, loud, quiet, sweet, bitter, fragrant, foul, soft, hard, smooth, rough, hot, cold, not_specified, other

Example: Exodus 15:23 — "They could not drink the water... because it was bitter." → Tag the water with bitter.

Example: Song of Songs 2:14 — "Your voice is sweet." → Tag the voice with sweet.

Character Properties

Moral and personality traits—who someone is on the inside.

Values: righteous, wicked, faithful, unfaithful, wise, foolish, brave, cowardly, humble, proud, not_specified, other

Example: Genesis 6:9 — "Noah was a righteous man, blameless in his generation." → Tag Noah with righteous.

Example: 1 Kings 3:12 — "I give you a wise and discerning heart." → Tag Solomon with wise.

Social Properties

A person's position in society—wealth, status, power, belonging.

Values: rich, poor, noble, slave, free, foreign, native, powerful, powerless, honored, despised, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 2:10 — Ruth says, "I am a foreigner." → Tag Ruth with foreign.

Example: Luke 16:19-20 — "A rich man... and a poor man named Lazarus." → Tag the rich man with rich, Lazarus with poor.

Emotional State

How someone is feeling at a specific moment in the story.

Values: happy, sad, angry, afraid, peaceful, troubled, confident, anxious, grieving, joyful, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:20 — Naomi says, "Call me Mara, for the Almighty has made my life very bitter." → Tag Naomi with sad or grieving.

Relational Properties

Life-stage status related to family and marriage.

Values: married, widowed, orphaned, firstborn, barren, pregnant, betrothed, divorced, not_specified, other

Example: Genesis 11:30 — "Now Sarai was barren; she had no child." → Tag Sarai with barren.

Example: Ruth 1:5 — "Naomi was left without her two sons and her husband." → Tag Naomi with widowed.

Material Properties

What something is made of—applies to objects, not people.

Values: wooden, stone, golden, bronze, clay, iron, silver, linen, leather, woven, not_specified, other

Example: Exodus 25:10 — "Make an ark of acacia wood." → Tag the ark with wooden.

Evaluative Properties

Value judgments—is something good or bad, pure or impure, valuable or worthless?

Values: good, bad, clean, unclean, holy, profane, true, false, precious, worthless, not_specified, other

Example: Leviticus 11:7 — "The pig... is unclean for you." → Tag the pig with unclean.

Quantity/Size Properties

Values: full, empty, abundant, scarce, whole, broken, deep, shallow, wide, narrow, not_specified, other

Temporal Properties

Values: ancient, new, temporary, eternal, first, last, not_specified, other

Spatial Properties

Values: near, far, high, low, inner, outer, right, left, front, behind, not_specified, other

Name Meaning (Special Field)

The meaning of a person's name, but only when the text activates it—through wordplay, explicit etymology, or narrative irony. Do NOT fill this for every name.

Example: Ruth 1:20 — Naomi (meaning "Pleasant") says "Call me Mara (Bitter)." → The name meaning is activated. Fill in: "Pleasant" for Naomi.

Example: Genesis 25:26 — "His name was called Jacob (meaning 'heel-grabber')." → The name meaning is activated by the birth narrative.

2. Participant Relations

Relations connect participants to each other. They answer: "How is person A related to person B?" Always specify direction (who → whom).

Kinship Relations

Family connections by blood or marriage.

Values: parent_of, child_of, spouse_of, husband_of, wife_of, sibling_of, grandparent_of, grandchild_of, ancestor_of, descendant_of, in_law, clan_member, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:4 — "Naomi's sons took Moabite wives." → Mahlon husband_of Ruth; Ruth wife_of Mahlon; Naomi in_law of Ruth.

Tip: Kinship relations are directional. "Naomi is mother-in-law of Ruth" means Naomi → Ruth with "in_law". Ruth → Naomi would also be "in_law" (from the other side).

Social Relations

Non-family social connections—authority, allegiance, friendship.

Values: ruler_of, subject_of, master_of, servant_of, ally_of, enemy_of, friend_of, teacher_of, student_of, colleague_of, not_specified, other

Example: 2 Kings 5:1 — "Naaman, commander of the army of the king of Aram." → Naaman subject_of the king of Aram.

Example: 1 Samuel 18:1 — "Jonathan became one in spirit with David." → Jonathan friend_of David.

Possession Relations

Ownership and control over things or people.

Values: owner_of, possesses, belongs_to, controls, lacks, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 4:3 — "Naomi is selling the piece of land that belonged to Elimelech." → The land belongs_to Elimelech (originally); Naomi possesses it now.

Spatial Relations

Where someone is from or where they live.

Values: located_at, native_of, inhabitant_of, from_place, near, far_from, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:1 — "A man from Bethlehem in Judah." → Elimelech from_place Bethlehem; native_of Judah.

Part-Whole Relations

How individuals relate to groups, or parts to wholes.

Values: part_of, whole_of, member_of, group_of, substance_of, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:2 — "Ephrathites from Bethlehem." → Elimelech member_of Ephrathites (a clan).

3. Non-Event Clauses & Modifiers

Not every clause describes an action. Some clauses simply identify, classify, or describe. These need different roles than action clauses.

Non-Event Roles

For verbless clauses (identification, classification, attribution, existential), use these roles instead of semantic roles like initiator/affected.

Values: subject, predicate, domain, location, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:2 — "The name of the man was Elimelech." → Clause type: identification. Subject: "the name of the man"; Predicate: "Elimelech".

Example: Ruth 1:2 — "Ephrathites from Bethlehem." → Clause type: classification. Subject: (the family); Predicate: "Ephrathites".

Evidentiality

How did the speaker or narrator know this information? This is important for languages that grammatically mark the source of knowledge.

Values: witnessed_visual, witnessed_sensory, reported, inferred, assumed, general_knowledge, divine_revelation, unspecified, not_specified, other

Example: Genesis 22:12 — "Now I know that you fear God." → God says this after witnessing Abraham's actions → witnessed_visual.

Example: 2 Kings 5:8 — "Elisha heard that the king had torn his robes." → Elisha learned by reported information.

Duration

How long does the event or state last?

Values: point (instant), bounded (has an end), unbounded (ongoing), not_specified, other

Duration Precision: exact, approximate, vague, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:4 — "They lived there about ten years." → bounded (ten years is a defined period). Precision: approximate.

Example: Ruth 1:3 — "Elimelech died." → point (death is instantaneous).

4. Discourse & Pragmatics

These categories describe how clauses fit together to tell the story—the flow, emphasis, and structure of the narrative.

Discourse Relations

How does this clause connect to the previous one? What logical relationship ties them?

Values: sequence, simultaneous, cause, result, purpose, condition, contrast, concession, clarification, elaboration, evidence, restatement, additive, resumption, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:1 — "There was a famine... so a man went to Moab." → The famine is the cause; the migration is the result.

Example: Ruth 1:1-2 — "A man went... The name of the man was Elimelech." → The second clause is an elaboration of the first.

Information Structure: Topic & Focus

Topic: What the clause is about (the "given" information). Focus: The new or important information.

Topic values: unmarked, marked_topic, contrastive_topic, not_specified, other

Focus values: unmarked, new_info_focus, contrastive_focus, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:3 — "וַיָּמָת אֱלִימֶלֶךְ" (And Elimelech died). → Elimelech is topic (we know him); "died" is new_info_focus.

Example: Ruth 1:5 — "וַיָּמוּתוּ גַם־שְׁנֵיהֶם" (And both of them also died). → "גַם" (also/even) marks contrastive_focus—emphasizing the tragedy.

Formulaic Marker

Is this a stock phrase or formula common in biblical literature?

Values: formulaic, non_formulaic, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:1 — "וַיְהִי בִּימֵי" (And it was in the days of...) → formulaic (standard narrative opening; cf. Genesis 14:1, Judges 17:6).

Peak Event

Mark exactly ONE clause as the peak—the climax, turning point, or most important moment in the pericope. Only one per pericope!

Example: Ruth 1:1-5 — The peak is Ruth 1:5: "Both Mahlon and Kilion also died." This is the lowest point that sets up the entire story.

Thematic Spine

Write a ONE-SENTENCE summary of what this pericope is about. This helps taggers in the Language Archive find similar stories.

Example: Ruth 1:1-5 — "A family flees famine but loses everything, leaving a widow alone in a foreign land."

Social Axis

In speech or interaction: who holds power? What is the social dynamic?

Values: equal_to_equal, superior_to_inferior, inferior_to_superior, divine_to_human, human_to_divine, formal_public, intimate_private, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 2:10 — Ruth bows before Boaz and asks why he noticed her. → inferior_to_superior.

Prominence

How important is this clause in the narrative?

Values: peak, high, medium, low, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:5 — "Both of them died." → Prominence: peak.

Pacing

How fast is the narrative moving?

Values: summary, normal, slow, pause, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:4 — "They lived there about ten years." → Pacing: summary (ten years compressed into one clause).

Focalization

Whose perspective are we seeing the story from?

Values: narrator_external (objective, from outside), narrator_aligned (sympathetic to a character), character_internal (from inside a character's mind), not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:20 — Naomi speaks about her bitterness. → character_internal (we hear her inner feelings).

5. Emotion & Source Tracking

These categories capture the emotional texture of the text and help us be transparent about how we know what we claim to know.

Emotion Type

What emotion is present in this clause? Consider the speaker, the affected party, or the narrator's tone.

Positive: joy, hope, gratitude, love, compassion, peace, trust, relief, awe, pride, contentment, desire, zeal

Negative: sorrow, fear, anger, disgust, shame, guilt, despair, anxiety, loneliness, jealousy, resentment, bitterness

Mixed/Complex: awe_fear, reverence, remorse, longing

Other: not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:9 — Naomi kisses her daughters-in-law; they weep. → sorrow, possibly love.

Emotion Intensity

How strong is the emotion?

Values: low, medium, high, extreme, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:14 — "They wept again" → high. Ruth 1:20 — Naomi says God dealt bitterly → extreme.

Narrator Stance

What is the narrator's attitude toward what is being reported?

Values: neutral (just reporting), sympathetic (with the character), critical (against), ironic (opposite of surface meaning), celebratory, lamenting, didactic (teaching), suspenseful, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:1-5 — The narrator reports the deaths without editorial comment. → neutral or lamenting.

Audience Response

What response is the text trying to evoke in the reader/hearer?

Values: empathy, sympathy, admiration, disapproval, fear, hope, relief, grief, joy, conviction, not_specified, other

Inference Source

When you tag something that isn't explicitly stated, record HOW you know it.

Values: lexical (word meaning), contextual (surrounding text), cultural (background knowledge), behavioral (actions imply feelings), physiological (weeping = sadness), speech_content, narrator_comment, reader_inference, theological, not_specified, other

Confidence Level

How confident are you in this interpretation?

Values: high, medium, low, speculative, not_specified, other

Example: Tagging Naomi with "sorrow" in Ruth 1:5: - Inference source: contextual + behavioral (she just lost her sons) - Confidence: high (it's clear from context)

Tip: Source Tracking is crucial for quality control. It forces you to ask: "Am I reading this into the text, or is it really there?"

6. Speech Acts & Figurative Language

Speech Acts

When someone speaks, what are they DOING with their words? This is different from what they literally say.

Values: - assertive — claiming something is true - directive — telling someone to do something (commands, requests) - commissive — committing to do something (promises, vows) - expressive — expressing feelings (praise, lament, thanksgiving) - declarative — changing reality by speaking (blessing, curse, pronouncement) - interrogative — asking a question - blessing — invoking good on someone - curse — invoking harm on someone - oath — calling God as witness - prophecy — speaking for God - praise — glorifying God or someone - lament — expressing grief formally - thanksgiving — expressing gratitude formally - petition — making a request (especially to God) - instruction — teaching - not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:8 — "Go, return each to her mother's house." → directive (Naomi is telling them to do something).

Example: Ruth 1:17 — "Where you die, I will die." → commissive (Ruth commits to staying).

Example: Ruth 1:17 — "May the LORD do so to me and more also." → oath (calling God as witness to a commitment).

Figurative Language

Non-literal language—when words mean something other than their plain sense.

Values: - metaphor — A is B ("The LORD is my shepherd") - simile — A is like B ("Like a tree planted by streams") - metonymy — Related thing stands for the thing ("The sword shall devour" = war) - synecdoche — Part for whole or whole for part - personification — Non-person treated as person - hyperbole — Exaggeration for effect - litotes — Understatement (negative of opposite) - irony — Opposite of surface meaning - idiom — Fixed expression with non-literal meaning - euphemism — Mild expression for harsh reality - anthropomorphism — God described with human features - rhetorical_question — Question that doesn't expect an answer - merism — Two opposites meaning "everything" ("heaven and earth") - hendiadys — Two words for one idea ("grace and truth") - inclusio — Bracketing repetition - word_pair — Traditional paired terms - not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:13 — "The hand of the LORD has gone out against me." → anthropomorphism (God described with human body parts) + metaphor.

Figurative Transferability

How easily can this figure of speech be understood across cultures?

Values: universal (most cultures get it), common (many cultures), limited (some cultures), culture_specific (needs explanation), language_specific (wordplay that doesn't translate), not_specified, other

Example: "The hand of the LORD" → common (many cultures personify divine action).

Example: "Jacob" meaning "heel-grabber" → language_specific (Hebrew wordplay).

7. Key Terms & Retrieval Tags

Key Term Domains

Some words carry heavy theological or cultural meaning. Tag them with their domain so the system can find validated translations in the Concept Bank.

Values: divine (God-words), covenant (agreement/relationship), salvation (rescue/redemption), sin (wrongdoing), worship (service/reverence), ethics (right/wrong), kinship (family), authority (power/rule), creation, land_belonging, not_specified, other

Example: Ruth 1:1 — "לָגוּר" (to sojourn/live as an alien) → Domain: land_belonging (theologically loaded term about belonging vs. alienation).

Key Term Consistency

Should this term be translated the same way every time?

Values: always (same word every time), preferred (usually the same), flexible (context determines), not_specified, other

Example: "חֶסֶד" (hesed / steadfast love / loyalty) → preferred (important to be consistent, but context may require flexibility).

Language Archive Retrieval Tags

Free-form tags that help match this clause with similar content in the Language Archive. Think: "What tags would help a tagger find a community story like this?"

Tag categories: emotion_tags, event_tags, register_tags, discourse_tags, social_tags, poetic_tags, parallelism_tags, proverb_tags, formulaic_tags

(Values are free text tags you create)

Example: Ruth 1:1 (the journey clause): - event_tags: journey_story, migration_narrative - social_tags: alien_sojourn, displacement

Example: Ruth 1:5 (the death clause): - emotion_tags: grief, loss - event_tags: death_narrative

8. Poetry & Wisdom Features

These categories apply mainly to poetic texts (Psalms, Proverbs, Job, Song of Songs, prophetic oracles) and wisdom literature. Skip them for narrative prose.

Parallelism Type

Hebrew poetry builds meaning by pairing lines. The second line relates to the first.

Values: - synonymous — B says the same as A in different words - antithetical — B contrasts with A - synthetic — B extends or completes A - climactic — B intensifies A - emblematic — One line is figurative, one literal - chiastic — A-B-B'-A' pattern - staircase — Repetition with addition - none — No parallelism - not_specified, other

Example: Psalm 1:6 — "The LORD watches over the way of the righteous, / but the way of the wicked leads to destruction." → antithetical.

Line Structure

How many parts does this poetic unit have?

Values: bicolon (two parts), tricolon (three parts), quatrain (four parts), monocolon (single line), strophe (larger unit), refrain, acrostic_element, not_specified, other

Sound Patterns

Values: alliteration, assonance, rhyme, rhythm_regular, rhythm_irregular, wordplay, onomatopoeia, tonal_pattern, not_specified, other

Compression

Values: ellipsis, gapping, terseness, normal, not_specified, other

Proverb Type

What kind of wisdom saying is this?

Values: observational (stating how things are), comparative (X is like Y), numerical ("Three things... four things"), rhetorical_question, conditional (if X, then Y), admonition (command + reason), consequence, characterization, not_specified, other

Example: Proverbs 10:1 — "A wise son brings joy to his father, but a foolish son is grief to his mother." → observational + antithetical parallelism.

Wisdom Function

What is the proverb trying to DO?

Values: teach, warn, motivate, evaluate, characterize, console, rebuke, celebrate, not_specified, other

Example: Proverbs 3:5 — "Trust in the LORD with all your heart." → motivate (encouraging a behavior).

Authority Source

Where does this wisdom claim to come from?

Values: tradition, observation, divine, elder, sage, unspecified, not_specified, other

Applicability

How broadly does this wisdom apply?

Values: universal, situational, cultural, contested, not_specified, other

Final Reminders

  1. When in doubt, choose "not_specified." Sparse accurate tagging is always better than dense speculative tagging.

  2. Use "other" sparingly. If you use "other" repeatedly for the same concept, tell your supervisor—it may indicate a gap in the ontology.

  3. Trust your reading. You were trained for this. If the text makes you feel something, that's data. Tag it and note your confidence level.

  4. The goal is retrieval. Every tag you add helps the system find matching expressions in the Language Archive. Think: "What would help someone find a similar story?"
